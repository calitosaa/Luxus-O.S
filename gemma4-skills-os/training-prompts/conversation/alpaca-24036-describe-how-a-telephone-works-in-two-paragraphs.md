## Asistente
A telephone is a communication device used to transmit and receive sound, usually in the form of a conversation. It uses a type of electronic circuitry called a circuit-switched network to enable two or more users to communicate with each other over a long distance. A telephone works by converting sound into electrical signals and sending them over a network of copper or fiber-optic cables. The electrical signals are then converted into sound by a handset placed near the user's mouth or ears. The recipient's handset then decodes these signals and reproduces the sound. Telephones also have other features such as caller ID and voicemail. 
 
Telephones typically consist of four parts – a microphone, receiver, transmitter and dial – connected to a network of cables. The microphone converts sound into electrical signals, which are sent to the transmitter. The transmitter sends the signal to the receiver via a network of cables. The receiver decodes the signal and then sends it to the speaker, which converts it back into sound. When the user dials a number, the dialing generates electrical impulses that are sent to the telephone exchange, which routes the call to the desired destination. Once the call is connected, the two parties can then communicate.
 
In short, telephones convert sound into electrical signals, transmit these signals over a network of cables, decode them at the receiver, and then reproduce the sound in order to enable two or more parties to communicate.
